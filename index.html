Management of Hypertensive Crisis

A working reference distinguishing hypertensive urgency from emergency — how to tell them apart, and how each is treated.

Hypertensive Urgency
≥ 180 / 120 mmHg
Severe BP elevation with no acute end-organ damage.
Goal: lower BP gradually over 24–48 hours using oral agents, usually as an outpatient or observed setting.
Hypertensive Emergency
≥ 180 / 120 mmHg
Severe BP elevation with acute, ongoing end-organ damage.
Goal: lower MAP by ≤ 25% within the first hour using IV agents in an ICU/critical care setting.

01 Telling Them Apart

The BP number alone does not distinguish them — the presence of end-organ damage does.

02 Signs of End-Organ Damage

Any one of these, in the setting of severe hypertension, reclassifies the patient as an emergency.

  • Neurologic: encephalopathy, altered mental status, stroke symptoms, seizures
  • Cardiac: acute coronary syndrome, acute heart failure / pulmonary edema
  • Vascular: aortic dissection
  • Renal: acute kidney injury, hematuria
  • Ocular: papilledema, retinal hemorrhages (hypertensive retinopathy)
  • Obstetric: eclampsia / severe preeclampsia

03 Nursing Assessment — First Steps

  1. Recheck BP manually in both arms, appropriate cuff size, patient seated/resting
  2. Full neuro check: LOC, pupils, speech, motor/sensory, visual fields
  3. Cardiac & respiratory assessment: chest pain, dyspnea, crackles, JVD, edema
  4. 12-lead ECG, obtain IV access, send labs (renal function, troponin, urinalysis)
  5. Fundoscopic exam if available (papilledema/hemorrhages)
  6. Ask about medication adherence, illicit drug use (cocaine, amphetamines), pregnancy status
Key point: Symptoms drive triage, not the BP number itself. Two patients can both present at 210/125 — one goes home on an oral agent, the other goes to the ICU on a nitroprusside drip.

04 Managing Hypertensive Urgency

No IV drips, no ICU. Oral agents, reassessment in hours, and same/next-day follow-up.

Avoid: Sublingual/rapid-acting nifedipine — historically used but linked to precipitous, uncontrolled BP drops causing stroke and MI. No longer recommended.
  • Reduce BP gradually over 24–48 hours, not in the ED in one sitting
  • Discharge teaching: medication adherence, low-sodium diet, home BP monitoring
  • Arrange follow-up within 24–72 hours

05 Managing Hypertensive Emergency

ICU admission, arterial line for continuous BP monitoring, IV titratable agents. The specific drug is chosen based on which organ is affected.

Nitroprusside caution: Effective but carries risk of cyanide/thiocyanate toxicity with prolonged use or renal impairment — monitor closely, limit duration.
The 25% rule: Reduce mean arterial pressure by no more than 25% in the first hour. Dropping BP too fast can cause cerebral, coronary, or renal hypoperfusion — the treatment itself can cause a stroke or MI if rushed. Exceptions: aortic dissection (reduce faster, target SBP ~100–120 within minutes) and sometimes eclampsia.

06 Nursing Priorities During Treatment

  • Continuous BP monitoring (arterial line preferred) — titrate IV drip per protocol/order
  • Neuro checks every 15–30 min initially; watch for new deficits (over- or under-treatment)
  • Strict intake/output, daily weights if heart failure involved
  • Cardiac monitoring/telemetry throughout
  • Have emergency equipment and reversal/backup drugs ready at bedside
  • Document trends, not just single readings — the trajectory of BP matters
  • Patient education begins even in acute phase: this sets up adherence after discharge

07 Quick Recall

Urgency = high BP, no damage, oral drugs, days to fix.
Emergency = high BP, active damage, IV drugs, ICU, minutes to hours, drop MAP ≤25% in hour one — except dissection, which needs faster control.
